## Artifical intelligence 

*For this experiment, I explored how cloud-based artificial intelligence tools can be used to interpret and represent public datasets about life in Aotearoa New Zealand. I selected the Traffic Management Levels dataset from the New Zealand open data catalogue because it reflects everyday patterns of movement and infrastructure within urban environments. Traffic management plays a significant role in shaping commuting, accessibility, and the rhythm of city life. Using ChatGPT, I engaged in an iterative dialogue to understand the dataset and generate visual representations, critically evaluating the AI’s outputs throughout the process*

## Dataset Selection

*I chose the Traffic Management Levels dataset as it provides insight into how traffic is organised and managed across different road segments. The dataset includes information such as carriageway identifiers, traffic management categories, and spatial attributes. I was particularly interested in this dataset because it connects to themes of rhythm, flow, and spatial experience, which align with my earlier experiments involving emotions, weather patterns, and kaleidoscopic visualisations. It offers a meaningful way to explore how infrastructure shapes daily life in Aotearoa New Zealand.*

## Understanding the Data with AI

*I uploaded the CSV file into ChatGPT to better understand its structure and meaning. Through a sustained dialogue with the AI, I was able to interpret the dataset, including the significance of the traffic management level categories and how they relate to vehicle volumes. The AI helped identify potential stories within the data, such as patterns of movement and the distribution of traffic intensity across the road network.*

*However, the AI initially focused on technical explanations and conventional visualisations, such as bar charts, without considering broader social or cultural contexts. It did not automatically address issues such as equity, community impact, or the lived experiences behind the data. These aspects required additional prompting and critical reflection.*

## Designing Multiple Representations

*1. Bar Chart Representation*

*The first representation was a bar chart showing the distribution of traffic management levels across road segments. This provided a clear and accessible overview of the dataset and highlighted which traffic categories were most prevalent. While effective, this approach reflected the AI’s default tendency toward conventional visualisations.*

*2. Spatial Interpretation*

*I then explored how the dataset could be represented spatially, considering how traffic management levels vary across different locations. Although a full map was not developed, this conceptual representation emphasised the relationship between infrastructure and urban geography, encouraging viewers to think about how movement is distributed throughout the city.*

*3. Narrative Interpretation*

*Finally, I considered a narrative representation that translated the quantitative data into a human-centred story about daily movement in Auckland. This approach highlighted the rhythms of commuting and the lived experience behind the numbers, demonstrating how data can be interpreted beyond purely statistical forms.*

## Critical Evaluation of AI Outputs

*Working with AI revealed several default tendencies. The AI initially proposed standard visualisations, such as bar charts with generic colour schemes and neutral titles. To produce more meaningful outcomes, I needed to override these defaults by specifying the intended audience and conceptual direction.*

*Among the representations, the bar chart effectively communicated quantitative information, while the narrative interpretation provided a more engaging and human-centred perspective. This process emphasised the importance of designer intervention in shaping AI-generated outputs.*

## Engagement with Course Readings

## Data Feminism — D’Ignazio & Klein 
*The principles of Data Feminism encouraged me to question whose experiences are represented in the dataset and whose are missing. While the traffic management data captures vehicular movement, it overlooks pedestrians, cyclists, and public transport users. This raises concerns about equity and whose mobility is prioritised in urban planning. The dataset reflects institutional priorities, highlighting the importance of critically examining power structures in data collection and representation.*

## Māori Data Sovereignty — Kirikowhai Mikaere
*Kirikowhai Mikaere’s discussion of Māori data sovereignty reframes data as a strategic asset for Māori development. Although the Traffic Management Levels dataset is not specifically focused on Māori communities, it prompted me to consider how infrastructure decisions may impact Māori land, accessibility, and wellbeing. Mikaere’s perspective emphasises the importance of culturally informed data governance and the need for Māori involvement in decisions about how data is collected, interpreted, and used.*


## Experience of Working with AI

*Working with AI was both insightful and engaging. I found it particularly interesting that AI can be understood as another form of coding, where prompts act as instructions that shape the output. This perspective helped me see AI not just as a tool for generating results but as a collaborative partner in the design process. I participated in the in-class AI activities, experimenting with how prompts could influence data interpretation and visualisation. Although I did not take screenshots during these sessions, the experience significantly informed my understanding of AI as a creative and analytical tool.*
